Dr. Frances Oldham Kelsey

"Her exceptional judgment in evaluating a new drug for safety for human use has prevented a major tragedy of birth deformities in the United States. Through high ability and steadfast confidence in her professional decision she has made an outstanding contribution to the protection of the health of the American people." --- John F. Kennedy

Dr. Frances Oldham Kelsey received the President's Award for Distinguished Federal Civilian Service in 1962.

  • Born on Vancouver Island, British Columbia.

    1914

  • Earned her master of science degree from McGill University, Montreal.

    1935

  • Earned her Ph.D. from the University of Chicago, and started to teach there.

    1938

  • Worked as an editorial associate at the American Medical Association.

    1950

  • Taught pharmacology at University of South Dakota.

    1954

  • Became a general practitioner there at University of South Dakota.

    1957

  • Moved to Washington, D.C., and began her career at the Food and Drug Administration. Took her stand against thalidomide during her first month at the Food and Drug Administration, on her first assignment, even though the pill had already been widely used in Europe and the manufacturers tried everything they could to get around her judgement.

    1960

  • Reports began to emerge in Germany and the United Kingdom that mothers who had taken thalidomide during pregnancy were now having babies with severe birth defects.

    1961

  • President John F. Kennedy awarded Frances Kelsey the highest honor given to a civilian in the United States, the President's Award for Distinguished Federal Civilian Service. She was the second woman to ever receive the award.

    1962

  • Retired from the FDA in 2005, at age 90, after 45 years of service, where she played a key role in shaping and enforcing the 1962 amendments. She also became responsible for directing the surveillance of drug testing at the FDA.

    2005

  • Died in London, Ontario at the age of 101.

    2015
